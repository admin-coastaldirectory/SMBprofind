What Are Restaurant Business Coaches?

Restaurant business coaches are educators who teach the operations, financial management, marketing, and leadership skills required to run a profitable food service business. Restaurants are among the most operationally complex small businesses — managing food cost, labor, inventory, customer experience, health code compliance, and online reviews simultaneously, often with razor-thin margins.

The restaurant education space covers a wide range of formats and business models: full-service restaurants, quick-service restaurants (QSR), food trucks, catering companies, ghost kitchens, and bar/nightlife operations. Each has different economics and operational rhythms. Toast's educational resources are particularly valuable because they're built on data from thousands of actual restaurant operations.

For restaurant owners, the most critical numbers are food cost percentage (typically targeting 28–35% of revenue), labor cost percentage (typically 30–35%), and the resulting prime cost (food + labor, ideally under 65% of revenue). Business coaches in this category teach how to build menu engineering, ordering systems, and scheduling practices that keep these numbers in control.

How to Run a More Profitable Restaurant

Menu engineering is one of the highest-leverage improvements any restaurant can make. It means analyzing which menu items are most profitable and most popular, then designing the menu to sell more of the high-margin, high-demand items. Price increases on popular items are often more palatable than owners expect — a $1 increase on a $14 item is a 7% price increase but a 50%+ increase in margin contribution on that item.

Labor scheduling is where most restaurants lose money they don't know they're losing. Building schedules based on projected sales (hour by hour) rather than fixed shifts eliminates overstaffing during slow periods. Modern POS systems like Toast provide sales forecasting that makes labor optimization dramatically easier.

Online reputation management is now as critical as food quality for restaurant survival. A restaurant's average Google and Yelp rating directly correlates with cover count. Systematically asking happy guests to leave reviews, responding professionally to every negative review, and monitoring your online presence weekly are non-negotiable for modern restaurant marketing.

Frequently Asked Questions — Restaurant Business

A healthy food cost percentage is typically 28–35% of revenue, depending on the restaurant type. QSR tends to run lower (25–30%); fine dining can run higher (35–40%) due to ingredient quality. Food cost above 38% generally indicates portion control, waste, or pricing problems that need immediate attention.
Reduce food waste through accurate inventory management (counting weekly), FIFO (First In, First Out) storage practices, daily prep sheets calibrated to forecasted sales, and cross-utilizing ingredients across multiple menu items. Toast and other modern POS systems include waste tracking features.
Toast is the most popular restaurant-specific POS system, with strong reporting, online ordering integration, and labor management features. Square for Restaurants is popular for simpler operations and food trucks. Lightspeed and Revel are strong options for full-service and enterprise restaurants. Toast provides extensive free educational resources on restaurant operations.
The highest-ROI restaurant marketing channels on a small budget are Google Business Profile (free, drives Google Maps and search traffic), consistent Instagram content (photos of food and staff), and proactive review management. Email marketing to a house list (collected at POS) is also highly effective for driving repeat visits.
Prime cost is food cost plus labor cost — the two largest controllable expenses in a restaurant. Prime cost should ideally be under 65% of revenue; 60% or lower indicates a well-managed operation. Tracking prime cost weekly (not monthly) gives operators the feedback loop needed to make quick corrections.
Lower-capital restaurant entry points include food trucks ($75K–$150K), ghost kitchens/cloud kitchens (no front-of-house buildout required), and pop-ups or farmers market stalls to test a concept before committing to a lease. Taking over an existing restaurant space with existing equipment ("second-generation space") also significantly reduces startup cost.
Ghost kitchens (also called dark kitchens or cloud kitchens) are commercial kitchen facilities that operate delivery-only restaurant brands with no dine-in space. They allow testing a restaurant concept at lower cost, and multiple brands can operate from the same kitchen. Delivery platforms like DoorDash and Uber Eats have invested heavily in ghost kitchen infrastructure.
Respond to every negative review within 24–48 hours — professionally, non-defensively, and with a genuine attempt to understand and resolve the issue. Never argue with a reviewer publicly. Offer to make it right offline (phone or email). A well-handled negative review often reassures prospective customers more than a string of unresponded positives.
