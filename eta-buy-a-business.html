What Is ETA (Entrepreneurship Through Acquisition)?

Entrepreneurship Through Acquisition (ETA) refers to the strategy of buying an existing, cash-flowing small business rather than starting one from scratch. ETA practitioners — sometimes called "searchers" — identify profitable SMBs for sale, evaluate them through due diligence, secure financing (often SBA 7(a) loans), close the deal, and then operate and grow the acquired business.

ETA has grown significantly in popularity over the past decade, driven by an estimated $10 trillion in SMB wealth transfer as baby boomer business owners retire. The model offers several advantages over starting a business: day-one revenue, an existing team, established customer relationships, and a track record that can support debt financing. The risk is different — acquisition, integration, and operational risk replace startup risk.

SMBProFind's ETA category covers the full acquisition lifecycle: finding deals (through brokers, direct outreach, and deal platforms), evaluating businesses (SDE normalization, quality of earnings, customer concentration), financing deals (SBA 7(a), seller financing, search funds, PE-backed models), and operating post-close. Educators like Walker Deibel, David Barnett, and Codie Sanchez each cover different corners of this landscape.

How to Choose the Right ETA Coach

The ETA space has several distinct paths, and the right coach depends on which one you're pursuing. Traditional search funds (where investors back a searcher with a salary to find a deal) are covered well by Harvard Business School's published resources and by educators like Michael Girdley. Self-funded searches (where the buyer finds and finances a deal without investor backing) are Walker Deibel's specialty. Codie Sanchez focuses on smaller "boring business" acquisitions that don't require institutional capital.

Also consider deal size. Acquiring a $500K revenue service business is very different from acquiring a $5M EBITDA manufacturing company. David Barnett is excellent for smaller deals ($1M–$5M sale price) and covers seller financing, deal structuring, and the nuts and bolts of buying a Main Street business. For larger deals, Michael Girdley and resources from the private equity world are more relevant.

Be cautious about educators who focus primarily on "how to get rich through acquisitions" without covering the operational reality post-close. The hardest part of ETA isn't finding or closing a deal — it's the first 12 months of operating a business you didn't build. Look for coaches who address integration, team management, and the real challenges of the operator role.

Frequently Asked Questions — ETA & Business Acquisition

ETA is the strategy of acquiring an existing, profitable small business rather than starting one from scratch. It offers day-one cash flow, an existing team, and an established customer base. The most common financing vehicle is the SBA 7(a) loan, which can cover up to 90% of the purchase price for qualified buyers.
An SBA 7(a) loan provides up to $5M in financing for acquiring a business, typically at 10% borrower equity, 10% seller financing, and 80% SBA-guaranteed bank debt. Rates are prime + 2.75% for loans over $50K. The business must have strong cash flow history and the buyer needs relevant experience. Walker Deibel and David Barnett both cover SBA financing in depth.
A search fund is a vehicle where investors provide capital to an individual (the "searcher") to spend 1–2 years identifying and acquiring a business. Traditional search funds raise $400K–$600K for the search phase, then raise acquisition equity when a deal is found. Self-funded searches skip the investor backing and use personal capital or debt-only structures.
Businesses for sale are listed on BizBuySell, BizQuest, and through business brokers. Direct outreach to business owners not yet listed for sale (proprietary deal sourcing) is how most successful searchers find the best deals — lower competition and more flexible sellers. Codie Sanchez's content covers both broker and off-market acquisition strategies.
SDE (Seller's Discretionary Earnings) is the most common valuation metric for Main Street businesses under $5M in sale price. It represents the total financial benefit to an owner-operator — net profit plus owner's salary, benefits, and any personal expenses run through the business. Most Main Street businesses sell for 2–4x SDE.
Buy Then Build by Walker Deibel is considered the foundational text for self-funded ETA. It covers the case for acquisition over startup, how to evaluate deals, SBA financing, due diligence, and post-close operations. It's highly recommended as a starting point before engaging any advisor or broker.
From first contact to close, most acquisitions take 3–9 months. Letter of Intent (LOI) to close is typically 60–90 days, covering due diligence, financing approval, and legal documentation. SBA loan approval alone can take 60–90 days. Building a qualified deal pipeline to find the right business can take 6–18 months.
Codie Sanchez focuses on "boring businesses" — laundromats, car washes, storage facilities, service businesses, and other cash-flowing Main Street operations that don't require tech expertise or startup experience. Her Contrarian Thinking newsletter and YouTube channel make ETA concepts accessible to a mainstream audience.
