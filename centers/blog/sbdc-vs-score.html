SBDC vs. SCORE: Which Free Business Resource Is Right for You?

By SMBProFind Team · 8 min read · Free Small Business Resources

If you're a small business owner looking for free expert help, you've probably come across two names: SBDC (Small Business Development Centers) and SCORE. Both are backed by the U.S. Small Business Administration. Both offer free services. And both can genuinely transform your business.

But they work very differently — and understanding which one fits your situation can save you weeks of frustration and get you the right help faster.

💡 Quick answer: Need a business plan, loan prep, or deep-dive consulting? → SBDC. Need ongoing mentoring from an experienced executive? → SCORE. Many business owners use both.

What Is an SBDC?

Small Business Development Centers (SBDCs) are a nationwide network of 950+ centers funded by the SBA and hosted by colleges and universities. Each state has a lead SBDC that oversees a network of local service centers — so there's almost certainly one near you.

SBDC advisors are typically paid professional staff with backgrounds in business, finance, or specific industries. They provide:

  • Free one-on-one business consulting (typically 1–3 hour sessions)
  • Help writing and reviewing business plans
  • Financial analysis and loan packaging
  • Market research and feasibility studies
  • Export assistance and international trade guidance
  • Government contracting preparation
  • Low-cost workshops and training programs

SBDC consultations are confidential and there is no charge for the one-on-one consulting. Some specialized workshops have a small fee.

What Is SCORE?

SCORE is a nonprofit organization with 250+ chapters nationwide, also funded in part by the SBA. Where SBDC uses paid professional staff, SCORE runs on volunteer mentors — mostly retired or currently working executives, entrepreneurs, and business professionals who donate their time.

SCORE offers:

  • Free mentoring sessions (in-person, phone, or video)
  • Ongoing relationships with a dedicated mentor (not just one appointment)
  • Business plan templates and financial projection worksheets
  • Workshops and webinars (many free, some low-cost)
  • Industry-specific expertise from mentors with real-world experience
  • E-learning resources available 24/7 on score.org

SCORE is entirely free — no fees for mentoring, ever.

When to Choose SBDC

Go to your SBDC first when you have a specific, time-sensitive need:

  • You need to prepare a business plan for an SBA loan application
  • You want a professional to review your financial projections
  • You're exploring a government contracting opportunity
  • You need detailed market research for a new product or location
  • You're looking to export your products internationally
  • You've just experienced a disaster and need help accessing SBA disaster loans

How to work with your SBDC:

  1. Find your nearest center at SMBProFind Resource Center or sba.gov
  2. Book an initial consultation — usually a 60–90 minute intake meeting
  3. Come prepared with basic financial info and a clear question or goal
  4. Follow up for additional sessions as your project evolves

When to Choose SCORE

Go to SCORE when you want ongoing mentorship and accountability from someone who's been in your shoes:

  • You want a mentor to check in with you regularly over months or years
  • You're looking for an executive with experience in your specific industry
  • You want candid advice from someone who has run a business — not just advised them
  • You're early-stage and need general direction more than a specific deliverable
  • You want someone to help you think through big decisions (hiring, pricing, expansion)

How to find a SCORE mentor:

  1. Visit score.org and search by location or industry
  2. Browse mentor profiles and find someone whose background matches your industry
  3. Request a meeting — first session is always free
  4. If the match isn't right, request a different mentor (no problem!)

The Smart Move: Use Both

These resources are not mutually exclusive. Many successful business owners use SBDC for project-based work (like loan packaging) while maintaining an ongoing relationship with a SCORE mentor for strategic guidance.

A common pattern:

  1. Month 1: SBDC helps you finalize your business plan and prepare a loan application
  2. Month 2–12: SCORE mentor meets with you monthly to review progress and help with decisions
  3. Year 2+: Return to SBDC for export assistance or government contracting; SCORE relationship continues

🎯 Pro tip: Ask your SBDC advisor and SCORE mentor to connect with each other. Some local ecosystems have established partnerships between SBDC centers and SCORE chapters for exactly this reason.

Other Free Resources to Know About

SBDC and SCORE are the two most well-known free resources, but the SBA ecosystem includes several more:

  • Women's Business Centers (WBC): Specialized support for women entrepreneurs, with 150+ locations nationwide
  • Veterans Business Outreach Centers (VBOC): Free counseling and training specifically for veteran entrepreneurs
  • APEX Accelerators (formerly PTAC): Help with federal government contracting
  • SBA District Offices: Direct access to SBA programs, loan referrals, and regulatory guidance
  • Chambers of Commerce: Local networking, advocacy, and often referrals to all of the above

Frequently Asked Questions

Is SBDC or SCORE better for startups?

Both are excellent for startups. SBDC is particularly strong for startups that need a concrete business plan or are applying for financing. SCORE is ideal for first-time entrepreneurs who need ongoing guidance and someone to hold them accountable as they launch.

Do I have to be in a certain state to use these resources?

SBDC centers are state-specific — you generally work with a center in your state. SCORE is more flexible; you can work with mentors anywhere in the country virtually, even if you prefer your local chapter in person.

Can I use both SBDC and SCORE at the same time?

Absolutely. There is no restriction on using multiple free resources simultaneously. Many advisors actually encourage it.

Are there income limits to qualify?

No. SBDC and SCORE services are available to any small business owner or aspiring entrepreneur, regardless of income, revenue, or business stage.

What if my SBDC or SCORE appointment isn't helpful?

Request a different advisor. The quality of individual advisors varies, and both organizations encourage clients to find the right match. Be specific about what you need and your industry — a specialist in your field will serve you far better than a generalist.
