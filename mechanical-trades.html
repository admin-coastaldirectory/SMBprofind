What Are Mechanical Trades Business Coaches?

Mechanical trades business coaches are educators who specialize in the business side of HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and related mechanical contracting businesses. They cover not just the technical craft — which trade schools handle — but the business skills that determine whether a talented technician builds a successful company or stays stuck trading time for money.

The best trades business educators on SMBProFind include HVAC School (Bryan Orr, one of the most trusted HVAC educators on YouTube), AC Service Tech (technical and business HVAC content), Electrician U (Dustin Stelzer, business coaching for electricians), and Roger Wakefield (plumbing business educator with 500K+ YouTube subscribers). These educators understand the specific math of a trades business — call volume, conversion rates, average ticket, technician productivity, and the economics of flat-rate vs. time-and-material pricing.

The mechanical trades also overlap significantly with the home services category. Tommy Mello (home services), Ken Goodrich (HVAC), and Mike Andes (lawn care, adjacent trades) all speak to challenges that mechanical trade contractors face. SMBProFind includes both in this hub for maximum coverage.

How to Grow a Mechanical Trades Business

Most trades businesses grow by solving four problems in sequence: more leads, better conversion, higher average ticket, and more repeat business. Leads come from Google Local Services Ads, strong Google Business Profile reviews, and referrals. Conversion improves with technician sales training and good-better-best pricing options. Average ticket grows by offering maintenance agreements and presenting complete system solutions rather than just fixing the immediate problem.

Hiring is the biggest constraint for most growing trades businesses. The shortage of skilled HVAC technicians, plumbers, and electricians is structural — demand exceeds supply in most markets. The best-run trades companies invest heavily in apprenticeship programs, technician career paths, above-market wages, and company culture to attract and retain talent. Ken Goodrich and Tommy Mello both cover hiring strategy extensively.

Software adoption is a significant growth lever that many trades businesses underutilize. ServiceTitan (enterprise), Jobber (mid-market), and Housecall Pro (small) automate scheduling, dispatching, invoicing, and customer follow-up. The businesses that implement these tools well typically grow 20–40% faster than those that don't. HVAC School and Tommy Mello's content covers software selection and implementation.

Frequently Asked Questions — Mechanical Trades Business

Starting an HVAC business requires state licensure (requirements vary), EPA 608 certification for refrigerant handling, general liability and workers' comp insurance, a van/truck, and basic tools. Most successful HVAC business owners worked for an established company first to learn both the technical and business sides. HVAC School and Tommy Mello cover the business launch process.
The most widely used HVAC software includes ServiceTitan (enterprise, $300+/month), Jobber ($100–$350/month), and Housecall Pro ($50–$200/month). All handle scheduling, dispatching, invoicing, and customer management. ServiceTitan has the most features and highest ROI for businesses over $2M revenue; Jobber is excellent for $200K–$2M.
Plumbing companies get customers through Google Business Profile (reviews), Google Local Services Ads (pay per lead), HomeAdvisor/Angi, and referrals. Roger Wakefield is the top YouTube educator for plumbing business growth and covers marketing strategies specifically for plumbers.
Flat-rate pricing presents customers with a fixed price for a job (e.g., "replace a water heater — $1,200") rather than charging time-and-material (hourly rate plus parts). Flat-rate pricing increases average ticket, improves customer trust, and makes technician compensation easier to structure. Tommy Mello and most home service coaches strongly advocate for flat-rate pricing.
HVAC technician wages vary significantly by market and skill level — typically $25–$45/hour for experienced technicians in most US markets, with top performers earning $80K–$120K/year including commissions. Commission structures tied to sales performance are common in HVAC and help attract and retain top technicians.
HVAC School is a podcast and YouTube channel run by Bryan Orr, widely regarded as one of the most trusted and educational resources for HVAC technicians and business owners. It covers both technical skills (refrigeration theory, electrical, controls) and business topics (pricing, hiring, customer service) with remarkable depth.
The most effective channels for hiring trades technicians are Indeed, Zip Recruiter, local trade school relationships, apprenticeship programs, and employee referral bonuses. Culture, career development, and above-market compensation are the strongest retention tools. Ken Goodrich and Tommy Mello both share detailed hiring frameworks for trades businesses.
Plumbing license requirements vary significantly by state and municipality. Most states require a journeyman's license (4+ years experience) plus a master plumber's license or contractor's license to operate a plumbing business. Always check your specific state and local requirements. PHCC (Plumbing-Heating-Cooling Contractors Association) is the authoritative resource for licensing information.
